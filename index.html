The Six Types of Socratic Questions

Each type serves a different purpose. Click to expand and explore examples tailored for agile product teams.

1

Conceptual Clarification — go deeper into what they mean

Go deeper

Get them to think more about what exactly they are asking or thinking about. Surface hidden misalignment in language the team thinks they agree on.

When to use in agile

Sprint planning, story refinement, whenever someone uses vague terms like "quality," "done," or "the process is broken."

Example questions

  • "When you say 'done,' what does that look like specifically?"
  • "What do we already know about this user's problem?"
  • "Are you saying we should stop doing X, or change how we do X?"
  • "Can you give me an example of what you mean?"
  • "How does this relate to what we discussed last sprint?"
  • "Can you rephrase that so the whole team has the same picture?"
Watch for: Teams often skip clarification because they assume shared understanding. Your job is to slow them down right here. The cost of aligning on words is tiny compared to the cost of building the wrong thing.
2

Probing Assumptions — shake the bedrock

Shake the bedrock

Make them think about the presuppositions and unquestioned beliefs on which they are founding their argument. Unexamined assumptions are where bad sprint commitments and failed products come from.

When to use in agile

Estimation, backlog prioritization, any time someone says "obviously" or "we always do it this way."

Example questions

  • "What are we assuming about how users will react to this?"
  • "We seem to be assuming the API will be stable — how can we verify that?"
  • "What if the opposite were true?"
  • "What would happen if we didn't build this feature at all?"
  • "How did we choose those assumptions?"
  • "And what else?" — the first assumption named is rarely the most important one
Watch for: The "AWE question" — "And what else?" — is deceptively powerful. Keep asking it. Their first answer is never their only answer, and rarely their best.
3

Probing Rationale & Evidence — show me the data

Show the evidence

When they give a rationale, dig into that reasoning rather than assuming it is a given. This moves a team from opinion-driven to evidence-driven — the engine behind "inspect and adapt."

When to use in agile

Sprint review (did we validate the hypothesis?), when someone advocates strongly for a technical approach, retrospectives when diagnosing root causes.

Example questions

  • "What data do we have that supports this priority?"
  • "How might we experiment to validate this before committing a full sprint?"
  • "Is this based on what users told us, or what we think they need?"
  • "Would that reasoning stand up if we presented it to stakeholders?"
  • "How do you know this? Show me."
  • "What evidence is there to support what you are saying?"
Watch for: This category is the Socratic engine behind empiricism. You're teaching the team to demand evidence from themselves — not from you.
4

Questioning Viewpoints & Perspectives — widen the lens

Widen the lens

Most arguments come from a particular position. Show that there are other, equally valid, viewpoints. This is arguably the most important category for team coaching because it breaks siloed thinking and builds collective intelligence.

When to use in agile

When the loudest voice dominates, cross-functional tensions (dev vs. design vs. product), retrospectives, stakeholder alignment sessions.

Example questions

  • "How would our end user describe this problem?"
  • "What would the ops team say about this architectural choice?"
  • "Who benefits from keeping things the way they are?"
  • "If we asked the stakeholder what success looks like, would they say the same thing?"
  • "What alternative ways of looking at this are there?"
  • "How could you look at this another way?"
Watch for: This type naturally extends into circular questions — asking person A about person B's perspective. Extremely powerful for surfacing hidden team dynamics.
5

Probing Implications & Consequences — then what happens?

Then what happens?

The argument they give may have logical implications that can be forecast. Do these make sense? Are they desirable? These questions pull teams into systems thinking — seeing downstream effects and ripple impacts.

When to use in agile

Technical debt discussions, prioritization trade-offs, when a team wants to take a shortcut, when they're avoiding a hard decision.

Example questions

  • "If we don't address this tech debt now, what will the next three sprints look like?"
  • "What are the consequences of that assumption for our users?"
  • "If this works, what does it make possible? If it doesn't, what have we learned?"
  • "How does this decision affect the other teams who depend on us?"
  • "Why is this important? What is the best outcome, and why?"
Watch for: Peter Hawkins' "Connecting" discipline in systemic team coaching emphasizes that teams must consider their impact on the wider system. These questions train that muscle.
6

Questions About the Question — go meta

Go meta

Turn the question in on itself. This is the meta-level — it interrupts unproductive patterns and helps the team become aware of their own thinking process. The most coaching-like of all six types.

When to use in agile

When a discussion goes in circles, when the team debates something that might not matter, when you want to build self-awareness about decision-making.

Example questions

  • "What's the real challenge here for you as a team?"
  • "I notice we've been discussing this for 20 minutes — what's making this decision hard?"
  • "Why do you think I'm asking you this instead of giving you the answer?"
  • "Is this the right question to be asking right now, or is there a deeper one?"
  • "What was the point of asking that question?"
Watch for: When team members start asking these meta-questions themselves, you know the coaching is working. You're modelling that the process of thinking matters as much as the conclusion.
